    What this test is

    Most personality tests ask how you behave. This one starts from what you care about. You rank the values that matter most to you, and the test translates that ranking into a personality profile: one of twelve archetypes, backed by a map of your motivations. It rests on three well-established ideas from psychology, woven together.

    🌱 ACT & values

    The {{ values|length }} values you rank come from Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), a modern, evidence-based approach to psychological wellbeing. In ACT, values aren't goals to tick off. They're freely chosen directions for how you want to live: qualities like compassion, curiosity, or courage that you can express in any moment. Starting here grounds the test in what actually drives you, in plain human language.

    🧭 The Schwartz circumplex

    Each value is mapped onto the Schwartz Theory of Basic Human Values, ten universal motivations (from Self-Direction and Achievement to Benevolence and Security) arranged in a circle. The circle has structure: neighbouring motivations are compatible, opposite ones conflict. Two axes organise it: Openness to Change vs. Conservation and Self-Transcendence vs. Self-Enhancement. This geometry is the engine of the test: it places you as a direction on a compass, and can even detect when your values pull in tension.

    📊 The Big Five

    The Schwartz map is precise but technical, so the test also speaks the language of the Big Five (OCEAN), the most validated model in personality science: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability. Every value also loads onto these five traits, so your result reads in familiar terms (“high Openness, warm Agreeableness”) rather than jargon.

    Why combine all three?

    Values and traits measure different things (what you find important versus how you typically behave), but decades of research show they line up in predictable ways. Combining them plays to each one's strength: ACT supplies a rich, behaviour-oriented vocabulary of values; the Schwartz circumplex supplies the geometry to turn a ranking into a coherent position (and to spot inner tension); and the Big Five supplies the everyday language to describe the person that position implies. The result is a reading that's both grounded in what you genuinely care about and easy to recognise as you.

    This is a reflective tool for self-understanding, not a clinical or diagnostic instrument.
